Understanding your forecast categories

Your forecast category tells your manager — and the business — how confident you are that a deal will close this quarter. Accuracy matters more than optimism. Here's what each category actually means, what belongs in each one, and how to use them well.

Accuracy at every step compounds. Your pipeline categorization drives your submitted total, which flows directly into the team number. The manager’s adjustment is tightest when your submissions are consistent and trustworthy — sandbagging and inflation both widen it.

Forecast categories explained
Commit commit

Your personal guarantee. You’re telling your manager: “This deal closes this quarter, full stop.” Commit is a promise — not a hope, not a stretch.

What belongs here

Legal review underway or complete. Decision maker engaged and has verbally confirmed intent. Next steps are agreed and on the calendar. Only an act of God stops this deal.

What doesn’t belong

Deals waiting on a champion to get internal buy-in. Legal hasn’t started. Anything you’d feel nervous saying out loud in a forecast call.

💡 Coaching tip
If you wouldn’t bet your own commission on it, it’s not Commit. A broken commit is a trust problem, not just a miss.
Probable probable

Strong intent, but not locked. You have genuine confidence this closes this quarter — there are just a few things left to resolve that are within your control.

What belongs here

Champion is actively building internal consensus. Pricing has been discussed and is in range. A path to close is clear — you can name the remaining steps.

What doesn’t belong

Deals where you haven’t spoken to anyone in two weeks. Close date is aspirational rather than agreed. Waiting on your prospect to re-engage.

💡 Coaching tip
The difference between Probable and Commit is usually one thing: a signed mutual action plan with a close date the prospect has confirmed.
Upside upside

Real opportunity, but timing is uncertain. You believe this deal could close this quarter if things break right — but it’s not something you’d stand behind in a forecast call.

What belongs here

Discovery complete and fit is strong. Prospect has expressed genuine interest. Meaningful obstacles remain — budget approval, legal, multi-stakeholder alignment — but you have a path.

What doesn’t belong

Early-stage discovery deals. Deals where the prospect has gone quiet. Deals that are honestly Q+1.

💡 Coaching tip
Upside is where you should be fighting hardest. Every deal you push from Upside to Probable — or Probable to Commit — is how you beat your number, not just hit it.
Pipeline pipeline

Qualified opportunities that are real, but not closeable this quarter. These are working deals — they’re not closed, and they’re not slated for this quarter.

What belongs here

Active deals where discovery is underway. Opportunities where you’ve confirmed pain and budget exists, but closing this quarter isn’t realistic or established yet.

What doesn’t belong

Zombie deals that haven’t moved in 60+ days. Deals you’ve lost but haven’t marked as such. Anything you’re afraid to move to Q+1.

💡 Coaching tip
A healthy Pipeline is your future quarters’ revenue. Dead deals in Pipeline hurt your conversion metrics and make your manager’s model less accurate — for you.
How to be a great forecaster
Best practices from top performers
1
Category should match your gut, not your aspiration
The most common mistake is putting deals in a higher category because you want them to close. An inflated commit that misses destroys trust. A conservative commit that closes builds it.
2
Update your categories after every meaningful interaction
Verbal confirmation of timeline? Move the deal up. Three unanswered calls in a row? Move it down. Your CRM is only as useful as it is current.
3
Know your next step for every Commit and Probable deal
If you can’t immediately name the next action, the next owner, and the next date — that’s a red flag. Deals without clear next steps slip. Your forecast call will surface this.
4
Multi-thread before it’s urgent
Single-threaded deals are the primary reason Commit deals miss. One org chart change, one vacation, one cold shoulder — and the deal slips. Build relationships with the economic buyer and legal early.
5
A mutual action plan is your close plan — use it
A MAP turns a verbal close date into a shared commitment. It externalizes the steps and makes slippage visible to both sides. If a prospect won’t agree to a MAP, that tells you something important about their intent.
